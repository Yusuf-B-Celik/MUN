Honorable Chair, Distinguished Delegates,  
The delegation of Albania wishes to highlight a danger that is often invisible but highly destructive: the digital front of this war.  
Modern conflicts are no longer fought only with traditional weapons; they are also fought in cyberspace. Russia’s cyberattacks do not just target military databases. They also target critical civilian infrastructure, hospitals, and humanitarian databases containing the sensitive personal data of millions of displaced civilians.  
Albania, having faced major cyberattacks against our own public systems in recent years, knows exactly how dangerous digital vulnerability can be. If a refugee's personal data or digital identity falls into malicious hands, their physical safety is also compromised.  
To answer the fifth question of our study guide, Albania suggests that NATO must expand its role in cyber defense:  
First, NATO’s Cooperative Cyber Defence Centre of Excellence should cooperate directly with humanitarian organizations to secure refugee registration databases.  
Second, we must assist Ukraine in building secure digital corridors, ensuring that civilian communication lines and banking services remain safe and functional.  
We cannot protect civilians physically if we fail to protect them digitally.  
Thank you, and I yield my time back to the Chair.
